The Fall of the House of Usher

Edgar Allan Poe — 1839

A pall hangs over the tale from its first breath: a nameless narrator approaches the House of Usher, and the mansion seems to watch him back. Its windows stare like vacant eyes, its stones sag as though exhausted by centuries of whispered sorrow. Even before he crosses the threshold, he feels it — that subtle, suffocating dread that clings like damp air in a crypt. Inside waits Roderick Usher, a childhood friend now hollowed by terror and illness. His nerves quiver at the faintest sound, his skin nearly translucent, as if the house itself has begun to drain him. His twin sister, Madeline, drifts through the halls like a doomed apparition, suffering from strange cataleptic trances that mimic death a little too convincingly. When Madeline finally “dies,” the siblings’ bond refuses to loosen its grip. She is entombed in the family vault beneath the house — a vault that groans with every storm. Roderick grows more frantic, convinced they have buried her alive. The narrator tries to dismiss these fears, but the house trembles with a rising, unnatural energy, as though it knows what is coming. Then, on a night split open by thunder, Madeline returns. Bloodied, staggering, driven by the final convulsion of life or vengeance, she collapses upon her brother. The twins fall dead together, their fates entwined as tightly in death as in birth. As the narrator flees, the House of Usher cracks along its ancient fissure. Stone by stone, it sinks into the tarn, swallowed whole — as if the earth itself is relieved to be rid of the cursed bloodline.
